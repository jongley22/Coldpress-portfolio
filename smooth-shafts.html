Smooth Shafts
Golf

A secondhand golf brand that needed to feel premium before anyone would spend $500 online.

HERO IMAGE — Smooth Shafts site screenshot or product shot
THE BRIEF

Smooth Shafts sells hand-picked secondhand golf clubs in New Zealand. Every club is individually selected, cleaned, and buffed before it's listed. Most orders go out with tees and a sleeve of balls — not because it's policy, but because that's just how they do things.

The problem was that none of that care was visible online. The previous site looked like any other secondhand gear listing. Someone landing cold had no reason to trust it, no reason to feel anything, and no reason to spend $400 on a driver they couldn't hold.

NEW SITE — after
THE WORK

We rebuilt the site from scratch — no templates, no shortcuts. The brief we set ourselves was simple: make the care visible before the customer has to take any risk.

That started with how the clubs are shown. Every angle. Every mark. Every sign of wear named and photographed honestly. Not to highlight damage — to remove doubt. When a buyer can see exactly what they're getting, the hesitation disappears.

We translated that same honesty into copy. The site's guarantee reads like a person talking, not a policy document:

"Every club we sell is individually inspected, cleaned, and tested before it's listed. All photos show the exact club you'll receive, and we intentionally photograph every mark, scratch, and sign of wear so there are no surprises when it arrives."

No stock photos. No hidden damage. No vague condition descriptions. What you see is exactly what you get.

THE RESULT

The Google Ads Performance Max campaign is still running at 4.2× ROAS against a 2.0× break-even. Every dollar in is generating roughly NZ$2.10 in gross profit after ad spend.

On Meta: 4.4% CTR on cold traffic. Industry benchmark sits at 0.9–1.2%. The ads were stopping people mid-scroll consistently.

5,089 sessions in 90 days. NZ$4,179 in attributed sales — and that's last-click only. Meta's real contribution is larger; the attribution model just doesn't capture it.

The checkout drop-off wasn't a website problem. It was a category behaviour problem — people comparing shipping costs to Facebook Marketplace. The creative did its job. The intent was there. That's a structural fix, not a creative one.
